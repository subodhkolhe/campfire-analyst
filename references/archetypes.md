# Consumer Archetypes from Investment Data

## How To Use This File

These archetypes are STARTING POINTS, not labels. Most people are a blend of 2-3 archetypes depending on the domain. Extract signals from the portfolio first, then see which archetype(s) match — don't force-fit.

**Every archetype has equal weight.** There is no "default" or "better" archetype. A Momentum Trader's predictions will be just as accurate as a Rational Maximizer's — they'll just be completely different predictions.

---

## Primary Archetypes

### 1. The Rational Maximizer

**Portfolio signals:**
- 50-100+ holdings across many sectors
- Long holding periods (low average buy prices)
- Holds through drawdowns without panic selling
- Systematic accumulation pattern
- Defensive allocation (gold 5-15%, some debt)
- Mix of direct stocks and ETFs
- No F&O / no leverage
- Both PSU and private sector holdings

**Behavioral prediction pattern:**
- Buys the best product within a mainstream price tier, never crosses into luxury for status
- Holds products for years (phone 4-5+, car 7+, watch indefinitely)
- Researches extensively before buying, then never revisits the decision
- Prefers reliability and durability over trendiness
- May show brand affinity within a country of origin (e.g., all Japanese durables, or all Indian brands)
- Outsources recurring services to professionals, buys products to keep forever

**Consumer tier:** Premium-mainstream. Best-in-class within the mainstream segment, but won't pay luxury premium for status.

---

### 2. The Momentum Trader

**Portfolio signals:**
- 10-30 holdings, frequently changing
- Active F&O positions
- Recent IPO stocks, trending names
- Short holding periods (average prices close to current)
- No gold, no defensive allocation
- High beta / small cap heavy
- Portfolio looks different every quarter

**Behavioral prediction pattern:**
- Upgrades products frequently (phone every 1-2 years, car every 3-4)
- Follows trends in both stocks and lifestyle
- Buys what's popular, reviewed well, socially validated
- Status-conscious — brands should be recognizable to peers
- Early adopter of new products and services
- Values being current over being optimal
- Active on social media, shares purchases and experiences

**Consumer tier:** Aspirational-premium. Willing to stretch for the latest and most recognized brands.

---

### 3. The Deep Value Investor

**Portfolio signals:**
- 20-40 holdings, heavily PSU / beaten-down stocks
- Very low average prices (bought during crashes or accumulated over years)
- High dividend yield stocks dominate
- No new-age tech, minimal premium consumer brands
- No international exposure
- High cash/liquid allocation
- Small portfolio relative to holding period (doesn't add aggressively)

**Behavioral prediction pattern:**
- Optimizes for maximum function at minimum cost
- Keeps products until they physically stop working
- Doesn't care about brands — cares about price-to-utility ratio
- Rarely upgrades unless forced by obsolescence
- Prefers local/domestic over imported
- Practical, no-frills approach to every purchase
- May be very wealthy but spends well below means

**Consumer tier:** Functional-budget. Cheapest option that does the job. Price is the primary filter.

---

### 4. The Aspirational Climber

**Portfolio signals:**
- 15-30 stocks, skewed toward "story stocks" and premium MNCs
- Holdings in HUL, Nestle, Asian Paints, Page Industries — aspirational blue chips
- IPO allotments in new-age companies (Nykaa, Zomato at IPO prices)
- Average prices often above current (bought at peaks during hype)
- SIP into mutual funds alongside direct stocks
- Occasional F&O attempt (usually unprofitable)
- Portfolio growing but not yet large

**Behavioral prediction pattern:**
- Buys entry-level luxury or premium-end mainstream
- EMI-friendly purchasing (stretches for aspirational brands)
- Follows influencer recommendations
- Cares about how purchases look to social circle
- Upgrading lifestyle as income grows
- Mix of premium and budget — splurges on visible categories, saves on invisible ones
- Active comparison shopper

**Consumer tier:** Entry-premium. Stretches for brand names in visible categories, optimizes in invisible ones.

---

### 5. The Conservative Steward

**Portfolio signals:**
- Legacy holdings (some stocks held 10-20+ years)
- Dominated by large-cap banks, insurance, and conglomerates
- Gold and fixed-income heavy
- Very few mid/small caps
- No tech stocks, no new-age companies
- Portfolio inherited or built very slowly
- Minimal portfolio changes year over year

**Behavioral prediction pattern:**
- Sticks with brands they know and trust for decades
- Family-oriented purchase decisions
- Traditional preferences — heritage brands over new entrants
- Suspicious of trends and new products
- Values longevity and proven track record above all else
- Purchases influenced by family, community, tradition
- Slow to adopt new technology

**Consumer tier:** Traditional-mainstream. Heritage brands, proven products, family-endorsed choices.

---

### 6. The Tech-First Builder

**Portfolio signals:**
- IT sector heavy (Infosys, TCS, HCL, or US tech via Nasdaq ETFs)
- New-age platform companies (Zomato, Paytm, Nykaa, Delhivery)
- International ETF allocation present
- Possibly crypto-adjacent or blockchain-curious
- Clean, organized portfolio structure
- Uses multiple brokerage features actively

**Behavioral prediction pattern:**
- Early adopter of technology products and services
- Ecosystem-driven choices (all-Apple or all-Google)
- Values efficiency, automation, integration
- Subscribes to multiple services (optimizes for convenience over cost)
- Digital-first in everything — payments, shopping, entertainment
- Global outlook — comfortable with international brands and services
- Likely in tech industry, consumes tech content

**Consumer tier:** Tech-premium. Pays for the best technology and ecosystem integration.

## Cross-Cutting Dimensions

These dimensions cut across all archetypes. Identify them separately.

### Dimension 1: Loyalty vs. Optimization

**Detect from:** Do they hold the same stocks for years? Do they use one broker or many? Do they consolidate or spread across multiple vehicles?

- **High loyalty:** Same bank for decades, same brands forever. Daily decisions driven by familiarity and comfort. May use a "worse" product because it's theirs.
- **High optimization:** Different provider for each need (bank A for savings, bank B for loans, bank C for credit card). Will switch for better value.
- **MOST PEOPLE ARE BOTH.** Loyalty for daily/emotional decisions, optimization for high-stakes/financial decisions. Detect which mode governs which domain.

### Dimension 2: Cultural Rootedness vs. Cosmopolitan Adoption

**Detect from:** Portfolio composition alone CANNOT reliably detect this. You MUST ask origin + current city.

- **Rooted:** Comfort food from home state, entertainment from childhood region, travel to culturally familiar places
- **Adaptive:** Adopts current city's culture, develops global palate, entertainment preferences shift with peer group
- **Most people are a MIX.** Same person can eat regional comfort food and international cuisine in the same week.

Regional cultural defaults (use after learning origin):
- Different regions have different entertainment preferences, comfort foods, travel patterns
- Don't assume — ask. But regional origin is a strong prior for cultural/emotional predictions.

### Dimension 3: Design Sensitivity

**Detect from:** CANNOT detect from portfolio. Only emerges in conversation when user rejects predictions based on aesthetics rather than function or price.

- If a user says "I didn't like the design" or "I'm waiting for a better-designed option" — this is a primary filter
- Design-sensitive people will veto products regardless of specs or price if the aesthetics are wrong
- Once detected, it becomes the FIRST filter for all subsequent predictions

### Dimension 4: Wellness Orientation

**Detect from:** Health-related holdings (hospitals, pharma), fitness/wellness companies, EV/sustainability holdings, systematic and disciplined portfolio approach

- **High wellness:** Pays for health optimization, fitness, organic food, ergonomic products
- **Low wellness:** Health is not a purchasing filter
- This dimension often appears alongside the Rational Maximizer archetype but can appear in any archetype

## Blending Archetypes

Most real people are a blend. Common combinations:

- **Rational Maximizer + Wellness Optimizer:** Buys quality durables AND invests in body/health optimization
- **Momentum Trader + Aspirational Climber:** Follows trends in both markets and lifestyle, status-conscious
- **Deep Value + Conservative Steward:** Ultra-frugal, traditional, price-is-everything
- **Tech-First + Rational Maximizer:** Buys the best tech but holds it for years
- **Aspirational Climber + Tech-First:** Stretches for premium tech, early adopter on EMI

**Identify the PRIMARY archetype (governs most decisions) and SECONDARY archetype (governs specific domains).** A person might be a Rational Maximizer for electronics but an Aspirational Climber for fashion.

---

## Critical Reminder

**DO NOT default to any archetype.** Read the portfolio. Extract the signals. Let the data tell you who this person is. Every portfolio is unique. Every person's lifestyle predictions should be unique. If you find yourself predicting the same brands for every user, you're doing it wrong.
